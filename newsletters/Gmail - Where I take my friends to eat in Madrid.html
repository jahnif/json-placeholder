Hi Joseph

It’s summer, and that means friends are visiting me in Madrid.

I’ll be honest: I always feel a twinge of anxiety when planning where to take them and what to do.

Specifically when it comes to where to eat.

I’m supposed to be the guy who knows all about that, right?

I mean, I’ve even written a ​Madrid travel guide.​​

But, dare I say, it worked out great this time around, and my friends who visited dined well!

So, this week, I thought it might be helpful to tell you exactly where I took them to eat and my reasoning behind each choice (because, yes, deciding where to take people to eat is an art form…). 👨🏼‍🎨

​

Day 1 - The neighborhood joint

Where: Restaurante el Montero de Cazorla​
​*I've linked to the one in Calle de Cipriano Sancho (which is prettier than the other one with the same name nearby).

Why: I always try to take friends to at least one tapas bar or restaurant in a neighborhood. It can be difficult for tourists to get out of the tourist center, so I like to give them a local experience.

Montero de Cazorla is in my neighborhood, and I think this place has good food.

It also has a varied menu (important for groups where everyone likes something different) and is just a really handsome old-school Spanish tapas bar.

Think jamón on the bar, fresh produce on display, and cool, old-school Spanish art on the walls. It just feels like Spain.

We ordered a bunch of plates to share and had a big, long, joyous dinner.

Day 2 – The tapas crawl

I decided to take my friends on a history walk through old Madrid and work a few tapas bars I like into the route.

Where: Stop 1 - El Anciano Rey de los Vinos​

Why: We started here for three reasons:

  • It’s right by the Royal Palace, so it’s a good start to our history route (Madrid was founded right where the palace now is).
  • They do a generous vermouth on tap (which all good tapas crawls must begin with…).
  • They serve a very delicious tapa called “bull tail surprise” (ask for un regalito de rabo de toro). It’s a juicy, delicious beef stew wrapped in pastry that always makes people swoon.

Where: Stop 2 - Casa Revuelta​
*I've linked to the original one, not the new outpost nearby

Why: After winding past the Royal Palace and taking them up via Plaza de la Villa, we head into Plaza de Puerta Cerrada.

Here, you can get the best version of a classic Madrid dish: salt-cod fritters (ask for una tajada de bacalao).

That might not sound delicious, but trust me—it’s melt-in-your-mouth good.

And all in a rustic, old-school, standing-room-only bar. It was a highlight for several in the group.

Where: Stop 3 - La Casa del Abuelo​
*I've linked to the original bar, where you can see them cook the prawns in front of you.

Why: Because they do the best damn garlic prawns in the city.

And I just love watching them cook this dish in seconds before my eyes.

Pair it with a “vino del abuelo,” a heady Alicante wine similar to what people would have drunk 100 years ago.

Note: You’ll sometimes see mixed reviews for this place. I think that’s because people go to the other outposts (there are a few in Madrid) and order a range of dishes. I only go to the original, and I only order garlic shrimp. That’s what they’re famous for.

Where: Stop 4 - Casa Toni​

Why: If you’ve been around these parts a while, then Casa Toni needs no introduction.

It’s just a fantastic, authentic, hole-in-the-wall, old-school tapas bar in a sea of touristy imitators.

Hearty, no-frills dishes cooked on the grill right behind the bar.

And a great atmosphere, with a mix of locals and tourists.

Day 3 - The flamenco evening

We took everyone to a flamenco show on the final night and finished with a two-stop tapas route for dinner.

Where: For flamenco, we went to Tablao Las Carboneras. ​

Why: As I explained in a recent post, there are several A-list flamenco tablaos in Madrid, and this one is consistently great.

Where: Taberna Tempranillo​

Why: My friends are into wine, and these guys have some good drops by the glass (and even more by the bottle).

We stood at the bar and ordered a selection of their tostas (think yummy things on bread), and they were a hit.

Where: Taberna Sanlúcar​

Why: I hadn’t given my friends a lot of veggies yet, and Sanlúcar has a wide selection of dishes from Cadiz (in southern Spain), including some lighter (less fried) options.

I love their patatas aliñás (a vinegary potato salad) and their salmorejo (a thick gazpacho).

Also, their presa ibérica (a piece of grilled black-hoof pork) is excellent.
