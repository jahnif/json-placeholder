Hi Joseph,

I've explored a lot of Spain.

But there’s one area that’s been on my bucket list for a long time

The Spanish Pyrenees.

Last week, I shared some photos from our trip, and this week, I want to share a bit more about our itinerary in case you decide to explore this fascinating and beautiful part of the country.

Because, trust me, it's quite possibly unlike anywhere you've been in Spain.

1. Spanish Pyrenees 101

So you know that the Pyrenees mountain range divides Spain and France, extending almost from the Atlantic to the Mediterranean.

Here’s a quick map...

What's more, the Pyrenees run through three Spanish regions: Catalonia, Aragon, and Navarre.

We spent most of our time in Aragon, specifically in the province of Huesca.

What's it like? Picture yourself wandering through peaceful valleys surrounded by towering peaks. Mountain streams wind through the ranges, cascading into dramatic waterfalls.

And it’s perfect for hiking or simply slowly exploring the remote alpine villages.

2. Where we went

First, let me be clear. We only explored a tiny section of the Spanish Pyrenees. So, I am not an expert. But if you know nothing about this area and expertise is relative, then consider me your Pyrenean guru 🤣.

We rented a campervan with all the mod-cons from a company in Madrid (not sponsored, but if you want to check them out, the link is here).

You don’t need a campervan to explore this part of Spain, but you do at least need a car.

The region is mountainous and isn't easily accessible via public transport.

In short, plan a road trip!

If you look at Google Maps, you’ll see that a fair chunk of the Spanish Pyrenees is national parks.

So, we focused our trip on the popular Ordesa & Monte Perdido National Park.​

Heads up, this part of Spain is kind of “trending” (if you’ll excuse the word). So, it was relatively busy in August with Spanish (and French) tourists. It’s not ‘Costa del Sol in summer’ busy, but we certainly weren’t the only ones up there.

Next time I go, I'd love to explore the area in spring or autumn. Perhaps staying in hotels, as even in August it often gets cool at night.

2. Ordesa & Monte Perdido National Park

We wanted to go on some semi-serious hikes on this trip, and we'd been told that Ordesa & Monte Perdido National Park has beautiful walking trails.

We bought a toddler backpack so I could carry Lucía (who still hasn’t discovered her love of long hikes, lol).

On our first night, we rolled into the village of Torla to find a campsite where we could park our campervan.

Many campsites don’t take reservations in August, meaning it’s literally first come, first served.

The first campsite, famous for its amazing pool, had a queue of 38 campervans ahead of us, which meant we weren’t going to get a spot.

But the next place, Camping San Antón, just up the road, had space.

And while it didn’t have an awesome pool, it did have a restaurant terrace with down-home cooking and breathtaking views.

From here, we took hikes, relaxed, and spent a few days soaking up the incredible surroundings.

To be clear, there are hotels in the towns. So you don't have to go to the camping route. But also, it's worth noting that the campsites often have bungalows if you're after simple accomodation.

3. Formigal & Sallent de Gállego

Next, we headed northwest, right near the French border.

Formigal is a famous ski resort town, but you can take the aerial tram up the mountain in the summer.

This gives you sweeping views of the valley below and the surrounding peaks.

And at the top, we experienced Lucía’s highlight of the trip: wild horses grazing in the alpine meadows.

Next, we headed for the town of Sallent de Gállego, near the French border.

And here’s the thing—these Pyrenean villages don’t look like what many people think of when they think of Spanish villages.

The stone walls, peaked roofs, wooden shutters…

You almost feel like you're in Switzerland.

Which just goes to show the incredible variety Spanish has to offer.

4. Monasterio San Juan de la Peña

By this stage, we were heading south, slowly winding our way home.

And we detoured to visit one of Spain’s most famous and dramatic monasteries, San Juan de la Peña.

It was built in the 10th century into a massive cliff face in a remote, mountainous area.

I found it fascinating how the founding of this monastery fits into the broader story of Spanish history.

As the Christians resisted the Moorish invasion in the north, monasteries like this were founded to solidify control over recaptured territory.

What’s more, legend has it that the chalice from the Last Supper—the Holy Grail—was hidden here to protect it from Muslim invaders.

It eventually ended up in the cathedral in Valencia, where it remains to this day (assuming you believe it’s the real one!).

The next day, we kept working our way south….

5. Loarre Castle

I'm a sucker for castles.

And the Castillo de Loarre is the best-preserved Romanesque castle in Europe and one of the most impressive in Spain.

You can wander through the chapels and halls, and the views of the valley below are breathtaking.

Again, this was built in the 11th century to consolidate power in the borderlands between Christian and Muslim Spain.

More recently, it was featured in the Ridley Scott film Kingdom of Heaven (now on my list of movies to watch).
