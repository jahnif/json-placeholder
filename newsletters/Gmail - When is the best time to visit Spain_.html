So, let's dive in, season by season!

Winter (December to February)

  • Head east and south: For milder weather, consider Costa del Sol, Valencia or Alicante. The Med softens the winter in these regions, and I have a few Madrid friends who like to head to the coast in February to get over the winter hump. Also, consider the Canary Islands for a mid-winter escape.
  • But it does get cold: While the east and south might be milder, mainland Spain does get cold in winter. So be aware if you're heading north, it may be nippy and rainy. You'll also need a coat in Madrid, but the winters are slightly drier than the rainy spring and autumn.
  • Christmas is a mixed blessing: Spain comes alive at Christmas as towns string Christmas lights and set up festive markets. But be aware there are also challenges: most restaurants close on the evening of the 24th, and all day on the 25th, then again on the evening of the 31st and all day on the 1st. So be sure you have your dining options organized over these dates.
  • It's true low season: Outside of the Christmas period (which is more or less 22 December until 7 January), these are the least touristed months, meaning you'll get cheaper hotels and feel like less of a "number" as you visit sights, try to get reservations in popular restaurants, or squeeze into bars.

Spring (March to May)

  • An ideal time: Spring offers mild temperatures, making it a great time to explore any part of the country.
  • March is a good month. Tourism numbers start to increase in April and especially in May, but March is a nice sweet spot. It's quieter, and the worst of winter is over (no promises on the weather, though!).
  • But the rain in Spain: The weather can be unsettled, especially in March. The week you're here might be hot or cool and rainy. But by late April and May, you'll have more consistent and stable weather.

Summer (June to August)

  • Beaches and heat: Spainâ€™s sun and sand tourism is in full swing in July and August. Consider visiting in June to enjoy the beach with fewer crowds and milder heat.
  • Coastal areas thrive: Coastal regions are bustling all summer, meaning everything is open to accommodate the influx of visitors.
  • Inland areas, not so much: Many locals head to the coast, making inland areas quieter than usual. Some shops and restaurants may close for two weeks to a month (especially in August). But these long closures aren't as common as they used to be, especially in areas popular with tourists.
  • Consider the north: Northern Spain offers a cooler alternative and is becoming more popular, though it remains less crowded than the south.

Autumn (September to November)

  • Increasingly popular: After summer, September and October are the busiest months, especially in cultural destinations like Madrid, Barcelona, and Seville. September was always Devour Tours' busiest month.
  • Unpredictable rain: Like in spring, rain can happen, but itâ€™s usually not severe enough to disrupt your plans.
  • November sweet spot: Tourism falls off a cliff in November. So, like March, it's a great shoulder month for avoiding the crowds, the Christmas rush, and truly cold weather.
