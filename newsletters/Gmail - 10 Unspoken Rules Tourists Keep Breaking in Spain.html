10 Unspoken Rules Tourists Keep Breaking in Spain

1. Be Assertive in Spanish Restaurants

In Spain, tapas bars and restaurant service prioritize efficiency over constant attention.

Spanish waiters often cover many tables, so they won't hover or top up wine glasses.

You'll need to be proactive, whether calling over a waiter for the menu, another drink, or the bill.

Remember: you often have to request the bill explicitly, or you might end up sitting with an empty glass longer than expected.

2. Tipping in Spain: No Pressure!

Tipping isn't compulsory in Spain, as waitstaff are usually paid a living wage.

If you want to tip because of excellent service or delicious food, you typically round up the bill.

3. Understanding Spanish Customer Service

But in neighborhood shops, restaurants, and tapas bars, tourists understandably often misinterpret brisk Spanish service as lousy service.

In busy Spanish establishments, the focus of customer service is swift and efficient, not necessarily overt friendliness.

This might seem off-putting at first, but understanding this can help you appreciate the machine-like efficiency of Spanish tapas bars.

Remember, a lack of small talk isn't rudeness; it's just a different way of prioritizing tasks.

5. Public Transport Etiquette

Proper etiquette on public transportation is highly valued in Spain.

Always stand to the right and let others pass on the left on escalators.

Elevators should be used primarily by those who need them (those with mobility issues, with children, or with suitcases).

7. Be a Good Neighbor

Many Spaniards dislike Airbnb apartments in their buildings due to noise from less considerate tourists and the potential safety concerns of constant visitors.

If you're renting an Airbnb in an apartment building, follow these guidelines:

  • Minimize Noise: Walls are often thin in Spain, so be aware of your noise level, especially during sleep hours. Be mindful of noise in common areas when moving in and out with luggage.
  • Respect Quiet Hours: Observe quiet hours, especially during siesta time in the afternoon (usually from around 4 pm to 7 pm) and late at night. This is particularly important on weekends and in buildings with older residents.
  • Proper Trash Disposal: Follow local guidelines for trash separation and recycling (and if in doubt, ask your host).

Spain Simplifies Tax Return for Non-Residents

Here's a quick tax update from leading Spanish tax attorney Federico GonzÃ¡lez.

Starting now, non-residents in Spain can simplify their tax obligations by declaring rental income annually instead of quarterly.

Previously, non-residents had to file quarterly returns within the first twenty days of January, April, July, and October for each rental property owned in Spain.

The new law, effective from the 2024 financial year, allows for annual declarations for each property rented.

The first declaration under this new system, covering all rental income from 2024, is due in January 2025.

Despite these changes, non-EU residents cannot yet offset income, deduct expenses, or apply for specific tax reductions available to residents.

Additionally, the requirement to file an annual declaration for real estate income for days the property was not rented remains in effect.
