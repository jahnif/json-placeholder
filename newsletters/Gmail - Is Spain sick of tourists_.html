Hi Joseph

As we head into summer (and tourism season), there have been several “anti-tourism” protests throughout the country.

Is Spain’s love affair with tourism over?

Below, I give you my perspective on these protests.

Plus, some of you might wonder if the anti-tourism sentiment extends to foreign residents setting up life in Spain.

I'll also discuss that.

Is Spain sick of tourists?

Google “over-tourism Spain,” and you’ll see a flood of recent articles.

Locals in many cities are protesting what they see as the tourist takeover of their homes.

What’s going on? Why the sudden uproar?

While many factors are at play, the main issue is housing. Spain hasn’t invested enough in social housing.

Plus, tourist rentals like Airbnb have skyrocketed, driving up rents.

This makes it difficult for locals, especially young people, to find affordable housing in historic and popular cities.

The result? Rising anti-tourist feelings in some parts of Spain.

It’s a tricky situation because tourism is a significant part of Spain’s economy.

Yet, the people working in tourism, like waiters, can’t find a place to live.

And this is the challenge balance.

As its economy is now structured, Spain (and Spaniards) need tourism.

The tourist euro has helped to revitalize certain parts of the country.

A friend from Malaga told me tourism has transformed the city’s historic center.

When he was younger, it was a seedy, no-go area.

Now, it’s one of the hottest tourist spots.

But the issue is the lack of balance.

One thing is revitalizing a part of a city; another is turning it into a tourist playground.

Protest organizers understand this, and their call is not to end tourism but for more controlled tourism.

According to an article on the website The Local, a leader from Cadiz’s “Cadiz Resists” movement said, “We’re not against tourists visiting our beautiful city, but we oppose uncontrolled mass tourism.”

Protesters are calling of a number of measures.

In Mallorca, they’re asking authorities to reduce the number of flights coming into the island, arguing that this is a key lever the local government can pull to ease the pressure.

In many other areas, locals are calling for better regulation (or prohibition) of Airbnb (and tourist rentals in general), which have exploded in number.

In parts of Malaga’s historic center, up to 40% of apartments are now tourist rentals.

My thoughts?

I believe that buildings that were built as homes for people should remain homes for residents.

Having a home is a fundamental right and something we shouldn't disrupt.

Spanish cities seem to agree and have begun cracking down on Airbnb (though with limits).

For example, Malaga just stopped issuing licenses for new tourist rentals unless the apartment has its own separate entrance (i.e., almost none of them).

Santiago de Compostela has also issued a similar moratorium on new tourist rentals.

When travelling, my family and I do choose “tourist rentals” (via Airbnb or Booking.com) if absolutely necessary, but usually only to stay in rural properties.

When visiting cities, Yoly and I prefer hotels and apartment hotels (the latter are great for families).

But a friend visiting Spain this summer highlighted a problem.

We’re meeting his family of four in Cadiz in July for a joint vacation.

Yoly, Lucia, and I are staying in an apartment hotel we like (if you're curious, this is the place). I sent the link to my friend.

He said it was too expensive for his family of four.

But he did find an Airbnb that fit his budget.

In other words, the laws of supply and demand suggest that eliminating tourist rentals will likely raise hotel prices, as has happened in New York.

Additionally, fewer tourists in Spain's historical centers would have trickle-down effects, leading to a shift in the commercial balance of these areas.

Businesses primarily serving tourists might pivot to cater to local residents (if local residents return).

I wonder if any cities worldwide have made this transition effectively – taking a step back from the over-tourism brink and "repopulating" their historical centers with locals.

Do you know of any?

Are Spaniards also sick of foreign residents?

​

If you’re curious about moving to Spain, you might wonder if the anti-tourism sentiment has spilled over into the anti-foreign resident sentiment.

As I mentioned in a previous newsletter, I did see some negative comments from locals about my recent Valencia video.

This is to be expected, given Valencia's popularity as a destination for people moving to Spain and the current housing tensions there.

However, foreign residents are generally not the focus of these protests and are largely off protesters’ radars.

I think the key is for those moving here to do their best to integrate, speak the language, and show curiosity about local culture.

I’ve always found Spaniards extremely welcoming and especially appreciative of my integration efforts.

When will the Golden Visa end?

I’ve been noticing a lot of these flyers in my neighborhood.

In short, it says a Chinese investor is offering to buy properties, for cash, in any state so they can get a Golden Visa.

As you may know, Spain has promised to scrap the Golden Visa, which allows you to get residency if you buy a €500,000+ property (or multiple properties totaling that amount).

I made this quick video about it on Instagram and TikTok.

I assumed foreign investors were trying to secure properties to get their Golden Visa before the residency route disappeared.

However, commenters have enlightened me that this is simply a real estate agent tactic looking to source properties.

Yet immigration lawyers have told me there is a rush to process Golden Visa applications before the law changes.

In many cases, these people had long planned to relocate to Spain but planned to do it a year or two in the future.

However, with the Golden Visa on the way out, they’ve moved up their plans.

Marina at Bureaucracy, the immigration firm I recommend for visa applications, told me they are still processing applications.

So, if the Golden Visa route was your plan for setting up life in Spain, it’s still not too late.

Marina also told me that we still don’t have any updates on when the visa will actually end.
