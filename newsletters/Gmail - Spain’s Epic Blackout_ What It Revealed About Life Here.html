Yesterday, in case you haven’t heard, Spain (and Portugal) experienced a near-total blackout from 12:30 p.m. into the early hours of the morning.

No electricity. No phone signal. No mobile data. In some places, it lasted over 12 hours.

I had a completely different plan for today.

In fact, I was supposed to be on a train to Valencia yesterday for a Move to Spain Masterclass alumni meetup — a casual get-together for course participants who have already made the move to Spain.

But, as the old saying goes, the gods laugh when you make plans.

And if you’re planning a move to Spain (or already living here), this blackout wasn’t just a piece of news. It was a stress test.

Because if the measure of a person is how they respond in a crisis, maybe the same applies to a country too.

So… how did Spain measure up?

🏫 The Official Response

This was never expected. Currently, we still don’t know the cause. Investigations are ongoing.

But as I followed along on the car radio, what stood out to me was the calmness of the institutional response.

(Plus, I realized that we need to buy a transistor radio.)

Here’s what I noticed:

  • Regional contingency plans kicked in almost immediately
  • Hospitals and prisons switched almost immediately to backup generators
  • Additional Police and Guardia Civil (in rural areas) were activated across the country
  • Prime Minister Sánchez gave two public addresses (6 p.m. and 10:30 p.m.)
  • Madrid’s schools opened this morning—not for classes, but so working parents had options (plus buses are free in Madrid today to help people get around).
  • No fatalities or medical emergencies caused by the situation were reported
  • Emergency services stepped up to meet the situation
  • Major train stations were kept open overnight so stranded passengers could sleep safely

🧑‍🤝‍🧑 The Civic Response

That's the institutional response.

But how did everyday Spaniards respond?

Around 5 p.m., Yoly, Lucía, and I went for a walk through our neighborhood.

And it reminded me of the early days of the pandemic... or the great snowstorm that hit Madrid a few years back.

Spaniards have this ability to meet crisis with community.

Yoly was driving when the blackout first hit. All the traffic lights went out. But people slowed down. They negotiated intersections with patience and hand signals. No blaring horns. No chaos.

Because while card readers and cash registers were down, the beer taps kept flowing. Turns out beer taps don't need electricity — good to know.

(Also note to self: always have cash on hand.)

Terraces filled with people, sipping drinks in the evening sun.

As we walked past, we kept hearing the soft buzz of transistor radios on tables and balconies — neighbors tuned into news updates, the sound spilling into the street.

Meanwhile:

  • People in our building, hearing we had a gas hob, were soon knocking on our door asking if they could come and heat up food.
  • Locals were asking if they could bring food to those stranded at train stations that remained open through the night.
  • I heard reports of taxi drivers helping people get where they needed to go without asking for payment.

There was no panic. No anger. Just a collective sense of we’ll ride this out together.

And late at night, when the lights finally came back on, there were hoots and cheers in the street — as if Spain had just scored the winning goal in the World Cup.

🛡️ Spain Held Steady

Now — I’m not saying Spain’s response was perfect.

I'm sure many countries would have handled this well.

And yes, it was “only” about 12 hours of disruption (albeit, total disruption).

And it’s worth noting that previous crises, like the devastating floods in Valencia, have at times revealed shortcomings in the institutional response.

But perhaps, like people, countries learn under pressure. Each challenge leaves a mark, and gradually, the systems get better.

And to the extent that this blackout offered a glimpse of how Spain operates under pressure — both institutionally and on a civic level — it was reassuring.

Life doesn’t always run smoothly. Wherever you live, there will be unexpected challenges.

But yesterday reminded me that, despite its imperfections, Spain feels like a place where, even in uncertain times, you can have some measure of trust the system — and the people — to come together when it matters.

As I send this out, 99% of the power has been restored.

The cause remains unknown. But the response? It said a lot.

And that’s the thing.

Yesterday wasn’t just a news headline — it was a lived moment.

A reminder that no guidebook or checklist can fully prepare you for real life in a new country.

When you’re planning a move to Spain, knowledge and community matter just as much as paperwork and logistics.
