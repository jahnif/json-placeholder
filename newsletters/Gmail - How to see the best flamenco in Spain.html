How to see the best flamenco in Spain

What is flamenco?

In short, flamenco is a traditional art form and music from Spain.

It developed in Andalusia over centuries and took its current form in the 18th century.

It principally involves singing, guitar, and dance.

Like jazz, the performances are largely improvised (around certain fixed musical structures).

And like blues, the lyrics are about lost love, hardship, and simple (often rural) pleasures.

Its origins are still murky, as there isn’t much documentation about early flamenco.

The Spanish Gypsy people were key to flamenco's development, but it is now believed that they weren’t its sole creators. It is believed that flamenco was an art form that grew out of the mix of cultures living in Andalusia over a number of centuries.

Initially, flamenco was performed in the intimacy of Andalusian homes amongst family members and friends, but it was in the 18th century that flamenco made its appearance in public spaces like cafés.

Should you see a flamenco show in Spain

I'm biased. But yes.

Even if you're not into dance or even into music that much, great flamenco is like a tense high-wire act.

It's full of drama and emotion, and, in short, it's beautiful.

It really is something you should try to experience when in Spain.

Where in Spain should I see flamenco?

OK, very broad generalization here...

It's generally said that the best flamenco in Spain is in Madrid. Which might seem odd, given that flamenco comes from the south.

Let me explain:

  1. Madrid has a long flamenco tradition, so there is a very strong flamenco culture in this city (and thus great artists).
  2. Madrid has long been where artists move to (traditionally often from the south of Spain) to make a name for themselves. So it's long attracted some of the best talent.

Having said that, Seville is also a great place to see flamenco.

And I'm sure you can see great flamenco in Granada and Barcelona.

But the two big 'flamenco cities' are Madrid and Seville.

Where do flamenco shows take place?

Nowadays, if you want to see a flamenco show, you generally go to a “tablao”, a venue specializing in flamenco performances.

They will have nightly performances (often multiple per night).

All the above cities have tablaos offering shows.

Are flamenco shows just for tourists?

In large part, yes. But that's not a bad thing.

The fact is that flamenco is quite niche in Spain. Not that many locals are into it.

So flamenco needs (and has long needed) tourism to survive.

That means that when you go to a tablao in Spain, most of the spectators will be tourists.

BUT...

You will also be seeing some of the best flamenco performers in the world.

Simply put, tourism is where the work and the money are.

So, as long as you go to a good show, you'll see great performances.

What should you expect in a flamenco show?

Generally, a flamenco performance in a tablao will include singing, guitar, hand clapping (an instrument in and of itself), and dancing.

Shows usually last about an hour and a half and generally include a drink in the price.

I would avoid getting dinner + show packages (where the tablao serves dinner while you're watching the show). The food is often (but not always) meh, and I find it a pain to have to eat in the dark while trying to watch the show.

So, I think shows are best enjoyed with a drink, and you can grab tapas for dinner before or after.

How do you pick a good flamenco show?

I think it's fair to say there are Tier A and Tier B shows.

The best performers will be at the Tier A shows.

(Having said that, you could also have a great night at a Tier B show; they may simply be younger or less experienced performers. But they still might be fantastic, up-and-coming stars!)

A combination of online reviews and price is a good indication of what's a Tier A show.

Reviews are pretty obvious. Look for a tablao with good Google reviews.

Then, for price, the Tier A tablaos in Madrid cost about €50 (show + drink), while they're cheaper in Seville (around €25).

OK James, but what are the shows YOU go to see?

Keep in mind that the performers in a tablao are not a troupe, and the shows are not fixed.

What that means is that flamenco artists are free agents. So the best ones will perform at all the top tablaos in a given city.

So it's virtually impossible to know which is the "best" show in a given city on a given night (as it could be any combination of artists).

Having said that, certain tablaos (the Tier A ones) are more consistently good.

My wife Yoly, a flamenco aficionado, generally recommends Las Carboneras in Madrid to friends. She's also heard good things about Flamenco de Leones.

And two good ones in Seville are Casa de la Memoria and Museo del Baile Flamenco.

I hope that helps you on your quest to experience great flamenco!
