Why this American couple swapped California for Spain

This article about an American couple moving to Spain got my attention.

It tells the story of a couple from California who sought a fresh start in Spain after some personal challenges.

But what was initially planned as a one-year adventure has turned into a semi-permanent move thanks to their new community, lifestyle, and the lower cost of living.

Their story is refreshingly honest about the motivations for moving to a new country and the trials and tribulations people experience.

So, I wanted to share what I think are five takeaways from their story.

I hope these are helpful for anyone looking at moving to Spain (or any new country!).

  1. Budget Realistically: The couple found living costs in Ronda were higher than anticipated but still lower than in California, highlighting the need for thorough financial planning. As best as possible, do a realistic budget before you move. List line by line your categories of expenses back home, and try to figure out what those will be in Spain. Check housing on Idealista.com, and when you're on scouting trips, talk to other migrants about what they spend, plus see what things cost in the pharmacies and grocery stores.
  2. Flexibility Over Assets: I love how they have chosen to rent rather than buy, keeping their options open. This is a good move for most people. Buying a home ties you down to a location (neighbourhood, region) more than renting. So, if you're planning to buy, consider putting it off for a year or two until you're 100% sure.
  3. Don't take the kitchen sink: It's a very personal decision what belongings you take when you move overseas. This couple whittled down their belongings to two suitcases. And while my evidence isn't empirical, my impression is that people generally wished they'd taken less, not more, when they moved to Spain. So be ruthless when getting rid of stuff (selling, donating, giving to friends).
  4. Build a community & learn Spanish: Before people move to Spain, generally their biggest concerns are, understandably, around visas and shipping, etc. But once they're here, I consistently hear that the biggest challenges are learning Spanish and building a community. I always recommend that people start learning (or improving their) Spanish WAY before they move. It'll make everything so much easier (and richer). And once you get here, you're going to have to work hard to make friends. Join in local activities and volunteer â€“Â it's literally like starting school again. Making friends takes work. But the effort is worth it.
  5. Spanish friends will come... with time. If you dream of a life in Spain with Spanish friends, speaking the language is essentialâ€”not fluently, but competently. However, don't be hard on yourself if Spanish friendships don't form in the first year. You may have more English-speaking friends initially, but as you build your community and improve your Spanish, your network will grow. Your move will go through phases, and the life you envisioned may take time to materialise. And that's perfectly fine.
