My Top 5 Transport Tips for Spain

1. Take fast trains, but understand the different companies

If you've been in this community for a while, you'll know that the fast trains in Spain are fantastic. They're reliable, clean and, well, very fast.

But previously they were expensive. However, the train system has been opened, and there are now four brands to choose from. Important: they are all as fast as each other. But some are low-cost (less frills), and some cost more and have more frills.

In short:

  • AVE: The original fast train. Full service with stewards, different classes, cafe car, etc. Has the largest network.
  • Iryo: The newest addition. Full service as per AVE above. They're currently offering GREAT deals.
  • Avlo: The low-cost AVE. It's still as fast, but just one class, no cafe car (vending machines).
  • Ouigo: Low-cost French operate. Again, it's just as fast, but there are two classes (sort of) and a cafe car.

Check out this video, where I review all four Spanish train companies.

To book, I recommend using Trainline. It aggregates all the fast train options so you can see all your options at a glance.

2. Rent a car ONLY if you're going to out-of-the-way places

If you're hitting the big tourist destinations (Madrid, Barcelona, Seville, Malaga, etc.), then all you need is the fast train. You don't need to rent a car. Getting in and out of these cities with a car can be a pain.

But, if you're going to be exploring off-the-beaten-path towns and villages, then renting a car can be a great option. It helps you better explore "deep Spain."

Consider even renting a car for just part of your trip, as and when you need it.

Don't forget that, technically speaking, to rent a car, you need an International Driver's Permit. This is effectively a translation of your license, and the rental car company MAY ask for it as well as your normal license. You can get them quickly and cheaply before you travel at your local AAA or similar.

A popular rental car aggregator website is rentalcars.com.

3. Regional trains & buses can be your day-trip saviour

It can be easy to forget that in between local Metro systems and the fast train network, there is also a network of regional trains and buses.

In Madrid, the regional train system is called Cercanias, and in Barcelona, it's called Rodalies. But there are regional trains throughout the country.

These can come especially in handy if you're looking to take a day trip out of a city. And it means you don't need to rent a car.

Take AlcalÃ¡ de Henares, for example. It's the birthplace of Cervantes and is just one hour by Cercanias out of Madrid.

While it's true that the difficulty level of using regional transport can be a little higher than using the fast trains or Metro, it's helpful to know that Google Maps includes these forms of transport.

4. Uber works... in some places

In Madrid (for example), there are taxis, Uber and Cabify. You can even price-shop between the three options before taking a trip.

If you want to use an app to call a taxi, it's FreeNow.

Uber doesn't operate everywhere. These are the cities where it does operate (in Spanish, but you get the idea!). So in smaller cities, your only option is generally taxis.

I don't use Cabify as much. But these are the cities it operates in.

Which is best? I find taxis are often a bit more expensive, but in Madrid, at least, taxis have access to the special taxi lanes, making them faster in rush hour travel.

If I'm going to use a taxi-like service, I generally use Uber. It's just so easy.

5. Feel the public transport fear and do it anyway

I get it; using public transport in a new city can feel daunting. But once you do it, you usually realise how easy it is.

The best way to get around big cities like Madrid and Barcelona is on public transport, plus walking.

It's cheap, fast and helps you feel more connected to the city than zipping around in a cab.

Then, in smaller cities (e.g. Seville, Granada, Malaga), all you need is walking.

Also, don't forget the local bike systems.

Several Spanish cities, including Madrid, Barcelona and Seville, have public bike-sharing systems. These can be an excellent way of exploring the city (just be sure to check first what the time limits are for each ride).
