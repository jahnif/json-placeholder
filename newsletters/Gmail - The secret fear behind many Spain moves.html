I was having coffee the other day with someone who’s thinking about moving to Spain.

We were talking about all the usual stuff—visas, where to live, the logistics of packing up your life and starting over.

And then she paused.

She looked at me and asked, “But will I be able to build community?”

It’s not a common question you see in the Facebook groups.

It’s not in the visa guides or the webinars.

But it’s there.

A quiet fear that lingers in many people's minds.

Because most people don’t just want to move to Spain for the weather or the food.

They want to slow down.

Spend time with people.

Feel connected to something.

And if that’s the vision—if the dream is to live more fully, more socially, more humanly—then building a community isn’t a bonus.

It’s the whole point.

But here’s the thing…

It doesn’t happen all at once.

There are phases.

Phase one?

You land, and you find your people—usually in groups of other foreign residents.

They’re warm. Welcoming. Full of tips.

But these groups are porous. People come and go.

It can feel a bit like the first day of school.

You meet the first people you come across, and maybe they’re not your people long-term—but they matter.

Because they’re the start of something.

And from there, it starts to evolve.

You get your feet under you.

Your Spanish improves (slowly, maybe awkwardly—but it does).

You start saying yes to things.

A drink after class. A neighbor’s invitation. A fascinating cultural event you don’t really understand.

And certain people start to float to the surface.

The ones who stick.

The ones who get you.

But it takes effort.

Take Toby and Lindsay, for example—alumni from my Move to Spain Masterclass.

They moved to Bilbao and just… said yes to everything.

They met people. Made friends. Got involved.

And now?

They’ve got a thriving social circle.

Toby even started the International Club of Getxo.

In short, he didn’t wait for the perfect friend group to materialize.

He built it.

Now, maybe you’re reading that and thinking, “Yeah James, that sounds great… but I’m not that extroverted.”

Totally fine.

Taking ownership of your community doesn’t mean becoming someone you’re not.

It just means recognizing that if connection is part of your vision for life in Spain—you have to be the one to shape it.

That might look different for every person.

But the key is showing up. Saying yes. Letting it take time.

And everyone's journey is different...

Let me tell you how it unfolded for me.

At first, I leaned on English-speaking friends. That’s where I was comfortable.

I also had Yoly’s network of Spanish friends—which, to be honest, made things easier.

Not everyone has that.

But over time, I developed my own rhythm.

I built up friendships in both languages. And I started to value the different types of connection each one offered.

With English-speaking friends, there’s an ease.

Shared cultural references. The same TV shows. The same jokes.

That kind of connection matters. It’s real. And it’s worth keeping.

But recently, I went on a trip that reminded me how far I'd come.

A weekend away in León with three Spanish friends.

Just the guys. All in Spanish.

We ate steak (the legendary chuletón at Bodega el Capricho, if you’re curious). We drank wine. We talked about nothing and everything.

And at one point, I sat there and realized something.

I feel like myself here.

Not a version of me translated into Spanish.

Not the guy politely nodding through a fast-moving conversation.

Just… me.

No anxiety about the language.

No self-consciousness.

I understood everything. I was in the flow.

And these weren’t friends I’d inherited through Yoly.

They were people I’d met, gotten to know, built something with—on my own terms.

And it felt… incredible.

I'm a perfectionist and I struggle to celebrate wins.

But in that moment, I felt proud of myself.

But there’s still a lingering question that comes up for a lot of people.

What about my friends back home?

Will I ever have friendships here that feel as strong, as deep, as easy?

I used to think I needed to replicate those old groups.

Find my Spain versions of my uni friends.

But that’s not how friendship works.

You can’t recreate those early bonds.

And here’s the good news—you don’t need to.

This isn’t about replacing anyone.

It’s about adding something new.

I shared a few photos from that León trip in a WhatsApp group with some old university mates.

The same familiar banter.

Same in-jokes we’ve been recycling for years.

That connection hasn’t gone anywhere.

But my life now includes them…

And these new people too.

And that’s the real answer to the community question.

Will you build deep friendships in Spain?

You can.

And when you do, they won’t replace what you had before.

They’ll add to it.

You’ll look around—maybe over an epic steak with friends you never imagined would become part of your life—and realize you’ve built something layered and wide and real.

A life that includes all the people who’ve shaped you—
The ones you grew up with.
The ones you just met.
And the ones you haven’t found yet.

Because if the life you’re hoping to build in Spain is about connection—not just location—then community isn’t a bonus. It’s the foundation.
