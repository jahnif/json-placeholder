Every October, sheep take over Madrid.

And it's a tradition I've never experienced first-hand.

But last Sunday, I had good reason.

I took my daughter Lucía to experience this fascinating and historically interesting festival.

Why are sheep roaming Madrid?

Every year, Madrid takes a step back in time to honour its pastoral roots.

It's called the Transhumance Festival, and sheep led by traditionally dressed shepherds make their way through iconic streets and plazas, all in a celebration of Spain's age-old practice of seasonal livestock migration.

The sheep enter the city via the Casa de Campo, Madrid's biggest public park.

From there, shepherds lead the flock through key parts of the city, usually passing by iconic spots like Plaza Mayor and ending up near the Puerta del Sol.

The streets come alive with bleating bells and flutes, giving you the feeling that you've stepped into a rural time capsule.

The festival celebrates the age-old practice of 'transhumance,' which was essential for the survival of Spain's pastoral communities.

Shepherds would move their flocks to higher, cooler pastures in the summer and head back down to warmer lowlands for winter.

This seasonal shuffle wasn't just about grass and weather; it's rooted in a network of legally protected drover’s roads, or 'cañadas,' that span hundreds of kilometres across Spain.

These weren't just dirt tracks but vital trade routes for wool, cheese, and other goods, linking rural Spain to bigger markets.

And it made me reflect on how Spain, at heart and historically, is so predominantly a rural country. It can be easy to forget those origins when living in or visiting the big cities.

But a big part of experiencing Spain and understanding this country is to explore its rural heartland and learn about these traditions.

In short, when you're thinking about visiting Spain, I would encourage you not to just stick to the big cities (Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia). Can you include some villages and small towns in your trip?
