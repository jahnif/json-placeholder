Barcelona is banning Airbnb

In November last year, Barcelona's new mayor said he would "significantly reduce" the number of tourist apartment rentals (including Airbnb) in Barcelona.

But last week, he went all the way.

What’s happening?

Barcelona is planning to ban all tourist apartments over the next five years.

By November 2028, the city will eliminate all 9,600 current short-term rental licenses.

Why is Barcelona banning Airbnb?

The primary goal is to make more housing available for locals.

By eliminating tourist rentals, the city hopes to add 10,000 homes back into the residential market.

This move is part of a broader strategy to address the housing shortage and make it easier for people, especially young adults, to find affordable housing in Barcelona.

The administration also hopes this ban will reduce the negative impact of mass tourism on local neighborhoods.

Many residents have complained about noise, overcrowding, and the overall disruption caused by short-term rentals.

By phasing out these tourist apartments, the city aims to restore a sense of normalcy and community to affected areas.

But why wait 5 years...?

The mayor has said that this is to indemnify owners of tourist rentals so they still have time to recoup their investment.

In other words, not to totally blindside them.

Also, I've read that this is to make the decision more difficult to challenge in court.

The current stock of tourist rentals isn't having their licenses revoked; they will just not be renewed after November 2028.

Are Barcelonians happy about the decision?

Many people in Barcelona have long complained about uncontrolled tourism impacting their way of life and altering the landscape of historic neighborhoods.

Undoubtedly, this will be positively received overall.

But not everyone is happy...

The association of tourist apartment owners, Apartur, has strongly criticized the move.

They argue that tourist apartments only make up 0.77% of the city’s total housing and that their elimination won’t significantly solve the housing crisis.

(I'd add that while that statistic may be true, tourist apartments make up a much higher percentage in certain historic neighborhoods).

Also, Apartur claims that tourist apartments account for 40% of the city’s tourist accommodation and that their removal could drive away family tourists (who often rely on Airbnbs).

This could, in turn, affect local businesses like shops, restaurants, and museums, potentially leading to closures and job losses.

They argue that the nearly 10,000 licenses support 5,000 jobs.

Will Barcelona become too expensive to visit?

I've received messages from people wondering how this will impact the availability of lower-cost accommodation or alternative types of accommodation (such as apartment hotels) in Barcelona.

It seems likely some hotel prices could rise (as they have in New York).

But the ban won't impact hostels or apartment hotels (the latter are a great option for families).

Others have suggested that more tightly restricting Airbnb rather than an outright ban would be better.

While that may be true, the experience in Spain so far has been that restrictions (or at least the enforcement of restrictions) haven't worked.

What do you think? Is this a good decision? Or has Barcelona's mayor gone too far?
