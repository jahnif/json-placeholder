Hola Joseph,

It’s late February, and you may be thinking (or dreaming!) about your next trip to Spain.

So, below are three articles highlighting three different Spanish cities – one well-known and two lesser-known – to throw into the planning mix.

But first, tapas.

On Saturday, we met friends at Cervecería Olivares in Madrid, which is about as “castizo” (i.e., traditional) as you can get.

It’s way outside the historic center, but if you’re the adventurous sort, it might be right up your alley.

If you’re on Instagram, check out this 360-degree video to get a better sense of the place.

But, enough of Madrid. Let’s head south!

Why do people forget about Cordoba?

A New Zealand friend is heading to Spain this year with his family. And he asked me for some help with his itinerary.

They have 10 days, and they’ve settled on Madrid, Seville, Granada, Cadiz (you can see my influence there), and Malaga (the kids want a day at the beach).

The itinerary makes good sense (in only 10 days, it can be tough to combine both the north and south of the country).

But it made me reflect on the fact that there is one city that is so often left off itineraries…

Cordoba.

Yep, it’s one of my favorite cities in the country.

Let me wax lyrical for a sec: Cordoba is one of Spain's most romantic cities. I still remember my and Yoly’s first trip there...

The mind-blowing Mezquita, the maze of whitewashed alleys and lanes, walls draped in vibrant bougainvillea.

And the food.

Yoly and I made this video a few years back, where we hit 5 fantastic bars.

(Note, stops 3 and 5 sadly didn’t survive the pandemic, but the other three – all classics – are going strong).

So, I was happy to see the city was recently featured in the New York Times, with an excellent itinerary of what to see, do, and where to eat.

I’m not much of a Michelin star guy (I find tasting menus a bit exhausting), but I would love to go to Cordoba’s Noor (which the article mentions).

Chef Paco Morales creates dishes inspired by the cuisine of Al-Ándalus (yep, no tomatoes, peppers, or potatoes, as they hadn’t arrived yet).

So if you’re planning your itinerary, and you have a spare night in Andalusia to play with (yeah right!), then definitely consider it.

On fast train, it’s 1 hour 45 from Madrid and Granada, and only 45 minutes from Seville.

Plus, come May, the city explodes with color for the patio festival (a busy, but breathtaking time to visit).

OK, I’ll stop ranting about Cordoba now, and let’s move (briefly) north.

Basque Country’s little-known capital

San Sebastian and Bilbao get all the attention in the Basque Country, but did you know its third city – Vitoria-Gasteiz – is actually the capital?

This article reveals how it’s long been at the forefront of ensuring access to nature and liveability for its residents.

Notably, Vitoria started pedestrianizing (or should we say re-pedestrianizing) its historic center in the 1970s, before that was even a thing.

And for bike fans, the city’s 180km of cycle paths is one of the most extensive networks in the country.

(The article doesn't mention it, but I also recall eating some excellent pintxos in the old quarter).

Following in Picasso’s footsteps in Barcelona

Finally, while Barcelona isn’t news to anyone, this article gives some clues for how to experience it from a different perspective (well, Picasso’s perspective).

Plus, it highlights a fascinating new book allowing you to follow in the artist’s early 20th-century footsteps.

I'll leave you to read deeper, and happy trip planning!
