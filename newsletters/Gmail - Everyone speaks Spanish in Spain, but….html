I’m sitting at a table in a very traditional Catalan restaurant, ready to order lunch.

The menu is in front of me—and it’s in a language I simply don’t speak or read (except for a few words).

We’ve just spent Easter up in Girona as a family.

It was wonderful—gorgeous stone villages, mountains that were vibrant green after recent rains, and this fantastic little restaurant...

Classic 70s décor, packed with locals, and a proper three-course lunch of regional dishes (with likely-rough local wine and a bottle of Casera to help the medicine go down).

My kind of place.

But...

Everything’s in Catalan.

The menu is in Catalan.

The waitress speaks Catalan.

The other diners around us are chatting away in Catalan.

And that brought me back to a question I hear again and again—from people in this audience and in my courses:

“If I move to a place like Catalonia or the Basque Country, should I focus on learning the regional language?
Or should I focus on Spanish?
Or… do I need to learn both?”

It’s a fantastic question.

Because learning a language is a big commitment. And when you’re preparing for a move, how you spend your time and energy really matters.

So let’s break it down.

Spain’s regional language setup

Spain has four co-official languages:

  • Catalan – spoken in Catalonia, the Balearic Islands, and parts of Valencia (where it’s known as Valencian; it’s essentially the same language, with some regional differences in vocabulary and usage)
  • Basque (Euskera) – spoken in the Basque Country and parts of Navarre
  • Galician (Galego) – spoken in Galicia
  • Aranese – spoken in the Aran Valley in the Pyrenees

These aren’t dialects. They’re fully distinct languages with their own grammar, vocabulary, and literary traditions.

And in many places—like the restaurant I was in—the local language is often the first language people grow up with. It’s what they speak with their families, their friends, their neighbours.

It’s the language of daily life.

So you might assume that if you’re moving to one of those regions, that’s the language you should focus on learning, right?

Well… not necessarily.

Because here’s the thing: Spanish is also universally spoken.

It’s co-official throughout the country, and—apart from maybe some elderly villagers in rural hamlets—pretty everyone speaks it (or, at least, can speak it).

In Girona, when it became clear I didn’t speak Catalan, people generally switched to Spanish without missing a beat.

I never had any problem being understood. No one seemed offended or frustrated.

You can absolutely get by in Spanish—even in places where the regional language dominates.

So should you focus on learning the regional language?

Let’s start with this: you can’t learn everything at once.

Most people don’t move to Spain fluent in Spanish, let alone Catalan, Basque, or Galician. Learning a language takes serious time and energy. You’ve got to be selective. Strategic.

And for most people, I think Spanish should be the first focus.

(If you’re preparing for your move and want to get your Spanish skills in shape, I highly recommend this practical Spanish program—it’s designed specifically for people getting ready for life in Spain.)

It’s the language that gets you through immigration appointments, supermarket trips, doctor’s visits, and chats with your landlord.

It works everywhere—from Seville to Santiago to San Sebastián.

But here’s the shift:

Getting by in Spain is one thing.
Building a life here is another.

Because eventually, you move past utility. You want to feel connected—to your community, your neighbours, your kids’ teachers, the guy who makes your morning cortado.

And in many places, especially outside the big cities, that means developing at least a basic relationship with the regional language.

Because language isn’t just a tool for buying bread. It’s culture. It’s connection. It’s identity.

So even if you’re focusing on Spanish, you’ll want to start building a working knowledge of key words and phrases in the local language—greetings, pleasantries, how to order a coffee, how to say thanks.

That effort—small as it may seem—is often what starts to turn a place you live into a place that feels like home.

But there are few caveats and nuances to this general advice...

Schools, jobs, and where you live

If you’re heading to a region with a co-official language, it’s worth looking beyond the broad label and digging into what locals actually speak in daily life in the specific part of the region you're moving to.

Barcelona and rural Girona are two very different experiences.

If you’re moving with kids and planning to enrol them in a public school, their education may be largely in the regional language.

Whether that’s a good fit will depend on your child’s age, their personality, and their existing level of Spanish.

Private or international schools may offer more instruction in English or Spanish, though they often come with a significant cost.

And it’s worth remembering—what the school says about its language model is one thing. You also have to consider what language are the kids speaking in the playground?

If you’re moving for work, particularly in public-facing roles or the education system, some positions may require knowledge of the regional language.
