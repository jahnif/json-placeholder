Hi Joseph,

So, what's new in Spain for 2025?

From high-speed trains to gastro celebrations, a lot is going on.

But it's also the year Spain tackles over-tourism. New tourist taxes and vacation rental rules are rolling out nationwide.

Plus, major changes are coming to how you enter Spain and Europe.

If you're heading here in 2025 (or you already live here), I hope this roundup from our latest video helps you plan.

(And stay tuned if you're moving to Spain. My next video will cover all the key changes for future residents.)

​

1. Discover the Most Loved Monument in Spain

Step aside, Alhambra and Sagrada Familia.

The most-loved tourist site of 2024 is the Mosque-Cathedral of Cordoba.

86.3% of visitors gave it 5 stars.

(Let’s just not talk about the practice of rating ancient and historic monuments out of 5).

It was built as a mosque in 785 on the site of a former Visigothic church.

After the conquest, the Christians built a rather gaudy (by comparison) cathedral inside.

Emperor Charles V ordered the change, but regretted it.

He famously told architects: “You have built what you could have built anywhere. But, you have destroyed something unique in the world.”

Nowadays, it’s transfixing to walk among the mosque’s maze of red and white horseshoe-shaped arches.

Travel Tip: Cordoba is under an hour from Seville by train, making it an easy day trip.

2. Tackling Over-Tourism: Airbnb Crackdown

Spain's tourism is soaring.

We got 94 million visitors last year, and 110 million are projected by 2040.

As a result, the government is cracking down on the likes of Airbnb.

In Madrid alone, 98% are operating without proper licenses.

The proposed solution: a national registry for vacation rentals. Every listing will need a government-issued registration number. No number? No listing.

The upshot is that supply could be restricted this year, driving up accommodation prices.

And keep in mind, this isn't just a Spanish initiative. It's part of EU rules all countries must implement by 2026.

3. New Tourist Taxes Across Spain

Tourist taxes are rare in Spain.

But 2025 brings several new fees as regions tackle overtourism:

  • Tenerife: €28 eco-tax for visitors to protected areas like Mount Teide.
  • Galicia plans a new accommodation tax between €1 and €2.50 per night (for up to five nights). Cities can choose to apply the tax or not, and so far, only Santiago has confirmed that it will.
  • Toledo: Considering €1.50 daily for bus tour visitors.

Catalonia and the Balearic Islands are raising existing taxes, while Valencia and Madrid continue to resist implementing any tourist tax.

4. Outdoor Adventures: New Hiking Trails

For nature lovers, the Balearic Islands are opening some exciting new routes in 2025.

Mallorca is launching the Gran Recorrido del Llevant.

This 104km coastal path runs from Cales de Mallorca to Cala Mesquida.

In Ibiza, seven new trails are opening.

They range from easy 10km walks to tough 50km routes.

Meanwhile, up north, Galicia, Asturias, Cantabria, and the Basque Country are creating Europe's largest ecotourism corridor.

This project will connect UNESCO biospheres and offer new hiking and nature activities.

Travel Tip: If you plan to explore these trails, download the Wikiloc app. You’ll find detailed user-generated route information for trails across Spain.

5. Benidorm Goes Green

Love it or loathe it, Benidorm just won "European Green Pioneer of Smart Tourism 2025."

They've reduced water use by 30%, and added solar panels to public buildings.

Native plants have been introduced to boost air quality.

The tourism board cheekily remarked that the award would "shut a lot of people up" regarding the city's sustainability efforts.

The city has also launched a smart app. It gives real-time updates on beach conditions, weather, and events to help prevent overcrowding.

Yoly and I visited a few years back and were pleasantly surprised.

In fact, we came up with our own slogan for the city.

​“Benidorm: Better than you think!”​

6. Major Changes at the Border

Two major changes are coming to how you enter Spain and Europe this year.

First up is EES (Entry/Exit System). It will replace passport stamps with biometric scans.

This is to prevent people from overstaying the 90-day limit within the Schengen area (which is like Europe, but a bit different).

Six months after EES launches, ETIAS kicks in.

If you're from a country like the US, Canada, or Australia (where you don't need a tourist visa), you'll need to apply online before traveling to Europe.

It’ll cost €7, take a few minutes to complete and will be valid for multiple entries into Europe for 3 years.

7. New Hotel Check-in Rules

But the new entry requirements don’t stop at the airport.

Spain has new data rules for hotel guests, including those in tourist apartments and campsites.

Hotels must gather more details about guests and upload this information to a government portal.

This includes full names, ID numbers, payment info, and, for minors, details about who the parents are.

The government says it's about enhancing security and preventing fraud.

But some privacy experts question how all this personal data will be protected.

8. Food Festivals Galore

Food lovers, start your engines…

Alicante has been named Spain's Gastronomic Capital for 2025.

The city will host various food-focused events throughout the year, including the National Tortilla Championship, tapas tours, and a dedicated cocktail week.

If you haven't been to Alicante city, this is your moment.

When I visited last year (here is the video), I was blown away by the vibrant food scene.
​

​
Then, head north to Catalonia, which has been named the World Region of Gastronomy 2025.

The region will celebrate with food festivals, wine fairs, and culinary routes showcasing its traditions.

While Barcelona is a natural starting point, this is a perfect chance to explore the beautiful (and delicious!) Catalan countryside.

9. Train Travel Gets Better (and Cheaper)

Spain’s high-speed rail network—the world’s second largest—is now more accessible than ever.

Prices are dropping, and budget options are expanding.

Iryo has launched Barcelona to Málaga in under six hours.

Budget operator Ouigo is launching trains to Seville, Málaga, and Córdoba, with tickets from just €9.

And Renfe’s new Spain Rail Pass makes city-hopping easy.

Spain's train system is now open to competition. This has made rail travel more popular than ever.

Passenger numbers surged from almost 24 million in 2022 to over 32 million in 2023.

Travel Tip: You can price compare all the Spanish train companies (and buy tickets) via the Trainline website.​

10. Murcia Turns 1,200

Never been to Murcia? How dare you!

And how dare I! Yep, I haven’t been either.

This southeastern city is marking an incredible milestone: its 1,200th birthday.

Murcia, founded in 825 by warrior king Abd ar-Rahman II, was renowned for its silk, paper, and ceramics.

To celebrate, the city has a clever plan.

Each month honors a different century of its history.

For someone like me—a Kiwi from the "New World"—it's mind-blowing to think of a city celebrating 1,200 years of history.

Maybe I’ll see you there?
