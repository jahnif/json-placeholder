Shepherds revive Spanish ghost village

In our recent video, Yoly and I shared our plans to explore the Spanish Pyrenees this summer.

This part of Spain is unknown to us, and we're eager to experience it slowly, traveling by caravan.

So, I was intrigued by an article about Spanish shepherds living sustainably in the mountains.

It tells the story of four people reinventing life in the nearly abandoned hamlet of Morillo de Sampietro, embracing sustainable shepherding and cheese production.

And the article captures some interesting ideas. I'll pull out a few quotes:

In 1860, the village had 76 inhabitants; by 1995, only two remained. Now there are four.

The village's decline mirrors Spain's broader rural depopulation trend, starting in the 1960s/70s as people sought employment in cities.

Yoly's parents moved from small towns with their families to Madrid in the 1970s, leaving a profoundly rural (and challenging) existence and finding work in the capital.

Nowadays, some people are heading back to the villages, looking to reconnect with a simpler way of life.

However, one of the shepherds is critical of how some are embracing rural life.

"There are lots of versions of rural life,” she says. “For me the most important thing is to respect the people who live here and be open to the knowledge they have and not come here like colonisers. People come here and work online but that isn’t living a rural life."

In the post-pandemic world, many of us yearn for nature.

I often wonder whether my family and I will always stay in Madrid or seek a place closer to nature, even part-time.

Currently, we're in New Zealand, visiting family.

Last night, Yoly and I enjoyed a walk along the waterfront, listening to the sea and surrounded by nature.

I would love more of that in my life.

Interestingly, the shepherd criticizes those who move to nature but remain tethered to their laptops, failing to engage with their environment fully.

While I find her view a bit harsh, it's certainly thought-provoking.

One of the shepherds also discusses eating meat.

"In Barcelona, I was a vegetarian because I thought it was the most sustainable way to live,” Aina says. “But as a shepherd I learned that it’s not a question of whether you eat meat or not, it’s about living on what you have.

We understandably equate not eating meat with a more sustainable way of living. Since I was told I had slightly high cholesterol last year, I've all but given up red meat for my health.

Plus, I know it's a better choice for the planet.

However, I thought it was interesting that the shepherd reframed sustainability around not only a meat/not meat choice but also about living within your (and the planet's) means.

I wonder if we could visit the village on our summer Pyrenees road trip. I'd love to learn more about how the four shepherds are getting on.

What are your thoughts on the questions raised in the article?
