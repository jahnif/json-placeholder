I walked a 2,000-year-old Roman road

(More photos after the text.)

This weekend, my friend Dermot and I walked 50 km (31 miles) from Manzanares el Real, in the Comunidad of Madrid, to Segovia, where we celebrated with a big plate of suckling pig.

In case you’re curious, this route is part of the “Madrid Way,” a branch of the Camino de Santiago (the pilgrimage route that ends in Santiago de Compostela).

It’s not a common path, but it was inspiring to see the stone markers along the way, letting us know how far we were from Santiago (now THAT would be a walk!)

We left the town of Manzanares el Real on Saturday around midday, walking about 20 km through meadows and woods.

That night, we stayed in the town of Cercedilla.

But Sunday was the big day.

We had 30 km ahead of us before we reached that plate of suckling pig, with the first 10 km taking us up and over the Guadarrama Mountains.

We set off in the dark at 5:30 a.m. and climbed for about two hours, much of it along the old Roman road.

This road once connected the important Roman towns of Mérida and Zaragoza.

Later, Spanish kings used it to travel between Madrid and their leisure palaces in La Granja and Riofrío.

It’s also in the area where Hemingway’s “For Whom the Bell Tolls” is set.

We descended the mountain into misty fields, with cowbells echoing in the distance, and arrived at Segovia’s ancient aqueduct by 1 p.m.

There, we treated ourselves to the famous suckling pig and a very well-deserved glass of wine (at the excellent restaurant José María).

I then took the AVE back to the Madrid, and made the entire journey in 26 minutes. 🤣

Golden Visa Likely To End in January ‘25

Spain’s Golden Visa has been on the chopping block for some months.

Remember, this is the visa that allows you to get Spanish residency (to live and work in Spain) if you purchase a property (or properties), totaling €500,000.

The country’s Prime Minister announced they’d be scrapping the visa in April this year.

Why?

Two reasons:

  • Spain’s housing crisis and lack of housing stock.
  • Fears the visa was being used to launder money.

Now, a report in Spanish newsletter La Región reveals that the visa will likely disappear in January 2025.

(The article is in Spanish. Translate it using the Chrome browser, right-clicking and clicking “Translate to English.” Or run it through ChatGPT.)

The article explains that there will be a transitional period, so anyone who has already requested the visa by the time the visa disappears will still be able to apply.

However, it doesn’t mention whether anyone who has purchased a qualifying property by the date the visa ends but has not yet started their visa application will still be able to apply.

This suggests that if you’re planning to purchase a qualifying property in the coming months, you should be very careful about the timing.

Hopefully, the government will announce the end date with lead time so people can move ahead with a purchase.

Plus, a reminder that people are still successfully applying for the visa.

Tax relief for those affected by flooding

If you live in Valencia or one of the other areas affected by recent catastrophic flooding, the Spanish tax authorities are offering some temporary relief.

In short, your payment of the second installment of personal income tax, due on 5 November, has been postponed until 5 February 2025.

This measure will apply to all territories declared as “Severely affected areas by a civil protection emergency,” which includes large areas of Valencia, Castilla-La Mancha, Andalusia, Balearic Islands, Catalonia, and Aragon.

If you live in one of the affected areas and have already paid the second installment, you can contact your bank, and they must reverse the charge within 3 days.

If you’re unsure, speak to your gestor or tax accountant.
