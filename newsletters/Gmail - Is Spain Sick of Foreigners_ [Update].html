A few weeks ago, I released a video called Is Spain Sick of Foreigners?​

You may have already seen it, but I wanted to follow up in email form—because the questions I raised in that video are still coming up again and again.

And I also wanted to make it easy for you to explore the links behind the video—so you can dive deeper into the context.

Since releasing it, there’s also been more news, such as the April 5 housing protests that took place across dozens of Spanish cities.

So I get it.

When you read headlines about protests, housing shortages, and graffiti calling out mass tourism, it’s natural to wonder…

Am I really going to feel welcome in Spain?

That question keeps coming up—in emails, in YouTube comments… and from participants in the current cohort of my Move to Spain Masterclass.

I’m hearing it from both travelers and future movers. People genuinely want to be thoughtful. But they’re also unsure. I’ve had folks ask:

  • What does it actually mean to be a respectful tourist?
  • Should I avoid Airbnb completely—or does it depend where I’m staying?
  • How do I plan a long scouting trip without making the housing situation worse?
  • If I’m relocating, is it okay to use Airbnb when I first arrive?

These are good questions.

And let’s face it—there’s no single answer.

I think it has to be about your attitude and overall approach.

It’s worth repeating: last summer’s protests weren’t about hating tourists.

They were about managing the impact of unregulated mass tourism—especially in places where short-term rentals have priced locals out of the market.

And the government has been taking steps.

There’s a national Airbnb licensing system, restrictions in many city centers, and efforts to crack down on illegal listings (right click the article in Chrome to translate it).

Still, the housing crisis continues, and that tension isn’t going away overnight.

At the same time, I’ve seen people assume these protests—or recent policy changes—mean Spain no longer wants immigrants.

Let’s be clear: this is one of the most pro-immigrant governments in Europe.

The Digital Nomad Visa was created to help people move here. Legal pathways for undocumented migrants have expanded. And the Prime Minister himself said:

“Spain needs to choose between being an open and prosperous country or a closed-off, poor country.”

The proposed 100% tax isn’t even aimed at people wanting to live in Spain—it’s targeted at non-residents buying second homes. And it’s just a proposal.

As for the Golden Visa, it was ended because of concerns around housing inflation and money laundering. It was a quick win for the government during a housing crisis. But most experts agree—it wasn’t really moving the needle either way.

So no, Spain isn’t shutting the door.

And people's comments in Facebook and Youtube suggest that the experience on the ground often looks very different from what the headlines suggest.

Here are a few examples I paraphrased in the video, based on what people shared with me:

“I was there in November. I saw the occasional anti-Airbnb sticker or graffiti here and there, but no issues at all.”
“Our neighbors brought us a lemon cake a few weeks after we moved in. Now we swap oranges and wine over the fence.”
“I’ve lived in Madrid for 8 months. 95% of people have been friendly. The rest? Neutral.”

And even if you’ve already watched my recent video, I really encourage you to go back and read the comments underneath.

These experiences—shared by people who’ve actually made the move or traveled recently—offer the kind of perspective you won’t find in a headline.

Here are two that stood out to me:

“I bought a house in a village 30 minutes from Sevilla. I recently spent a week at my new home getting things ready for my permanent move later this year. The first thing I did was introduce myself to my neighbors who warmly welcomed me to their community. They intently listened to my story as to why I decided to move to Spain and my connection through my Spanish grandparents who immigrated from Spain to Cuba over 100 years ago. Spanish villages are a great place to live if you want a full immersion into Spanish culture and language.”
“I’ve nothing to complain about since my move to Spain back in 2023. Among a population of 8000 people we do happen to have 42 different nationalities. Our mayor was so pleased with the idea of having so many cultures living together, that she decided to create a 2-days festival ‘Hi Olvera Nations Fest’. Everyone is hoping for a second edition.”

I’m also working on a new video about how to ethically visit Spain (or anywhere).

I’ve gotten some fantastic suggestions from many of you, some of which I shared in this Instagram reel, and I’ll be weaving more into the final version.

Here are a few that really stuck with me:

“Don’t go in high season if you can. Go in the off season when there’s unfilled capacity in hotels and restaurants. That way, these places can survive throughout the year.”
“My husband always takes a refresher course on the language for 2 weeks before we fly to a place.”
“When I was in Gran Canaria recently, I made sure to order the local dishes: gofio, papas arrugadas, to make sure I showed the restaurants—even the touristy ones—that they were worth keeping on the menu.”

(It's also worth reading the replies to the reel for even more ideas.)

None of these things are complicated. But together, they add up.

Because I don't think being a respectful traveler—or a thoughtful new resident—isn’t about being perfect.

It’s about showing up with humility, curiosity, and care.

And in my experience, when you approach Spain with respect and curiosity, you’ll be met with the same.
