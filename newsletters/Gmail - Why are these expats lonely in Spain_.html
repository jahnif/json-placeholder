Trouble in Paradise: Loneliness and Isolation in Spain

This recent article sheds light on how isolation and loneliness can affect those who relocate to Spain.

While it presents various reasons for this, one reason stuck out for me: The challenges of learning Spanish.

Although the article focuses on British migrants, the insights are applicable to people from any country.

Living in Spain without a functional level of Spanish can create a chasm, leading to isolation and a superficial experience of the country's rich culture.

I see a lot of people who move to Spain saying that, in retrospect, they wished they'd focused more on learning Spanish before they made the move.

I was in the same boat.

When I moved to Spain, I had pretty limited Spanish. And while I did work hard to pick it up, my language-learning journey was slower than it should have been.

This led to situations where I felt like I wasn't plugged into what was happening around me or couldn't feel fully at ease in certain social situations.

If I had my time again, I would start learning Spanish the moment Yoly and I decided we were moving to Spain (about a year before the move).

But here's the $1,000,000 question. What's the best way to learn Spanish as an adult?

A lot of people rely on apps like Duolingo. They're fun(ish) and are designed to give you the impression that you're learning the language effectively.

But I've long suspected that the apps alone don't cut it.

And finally, I've figured out why language learning apps can't be relied on alone.

It's because apps, like reading books or listening to music, are all tools to help you learn Spanish.

Tools are the essential building blocks, but if your language learning plan only consists of tools, it's missing the most critical component.

A study plan.

You need a study plan designed specifically for your Spanish learning needs.

Do you need Spanish to set up a life in Spain (i.e., make friends, deal with the doctor)? Do you need Spanish to do business in Spain? Or do you need Spanish to travel as a tourist through Spain?

Depending on your goal, you'll need a different plan.

Tools like apps or watching Netflix in Spanish are great for practicing. But they'll be much more effective when used as part of a study plan.

Otherwise, you're effectively driving blind. Yeah, you'll earn some stuff, but it's going to be slow going and a little directionless.

So, you need to think of the study plan as the roof. All then, all the apps and tools slot in underneath it.

This structured approach ensures steady progress and a deeper understanding of the language.

And trust me, I'm talking from hard-earned experience.

I didn't start working with a Spanish tutor (who gave me a study plan) until about a year ago. Way too late. Yes, my Spanish was already advanced. However, I had hit a plateau and I had gaps in my Spanish knowledge (did I mention the damned subjunctive tense?)

After a year of classes with my Spanish tutor, Alicia, I am more confident in social situations and less hesitant when using some common but complex grammatical structures.
