Barcelona’s bold new approach to over-tourism

Ever since the 1992 Olympics, when the world “discovered” Barcelona, it’s gone from strength to strength as a tourism destination.

However, this success has also put the city at the forefront of Spain’s challenges with mass tourism.

Barcelona, often a trendsetter, has recently taken a dramatic step by announcing a ban on Airbnb starting in 2029.

Now, they’ve made a more subtle yet telling change to their tourism slogan.

Instead of “Visit Barcelona,” it’s now “This is Barcelona.”

A recent promotional video suggests the city is shifting focus away from traditional tourism images—beach, food, fiesta—and more towards lesser-known cultural events and activities (well, and the America’s Cup).

According to this article,

Barcelona is moving to attract ‘higher quality’ visitors – which mean tourists who spend more money and take time to discover a destination sustainably.

I’m uneasy with the term ‘higher quality’ visitors, as it’s often a euphemism for wealthier tourists and seems to imply that only those with money are interested in culture.

But I understand the city (and its inhabitants) want to move away from being seen as a more hedonistic destination.

Overall, I think it’s a smart move to reposition Barcelona as a more sophisticated destination.

However, I’ll admit I’m not a particularly sophisticated traveler. I usually seek out experiences that are a bit more down-to-earth.

If you’re like me, here’s a suggestion: on your next visit to Barcelona, think of the city as a gateway to the fascinating region of Catalonia. Frame your trip around exploring Catalan history, culture, and cuisine.

The book “Barcelona” by Robert Hughes, though slightly dated now, is an excellent primer.

Consider taking a civil war tour of the city.

Before traveling, read up on local food so you can spot regional dishes on menus (I haven’t read this book, but it looks interesting).

Keep in mind that Catalonia is home to 10 different wine regions. Try ordering wines from these areas (instead of the classic Rioja).

(For the history buffs: here’s a fascinating tidbit from Wikipedia— "Recovered pieces of amphora indicate that the Phoenicians traded ancient Catalan wines with the Egyptians.” Now that’s history!)

Yes, I love exploring the historic Born and Gothic neighborhoods, but beautiful as they are, they can feel a bit like Disneyland these days.

By venturing deeper and wider, you might discover a new side of Barcelona you didn’t expect.

(I’ll step off my soapbox now.)

In short, while the peak of summer protests may have passed, I expect we’ll see more subtle shifts during the off-season in how key Spanish cities promote and brand themselves to tourists.

It reminds me of a billboard I saw for the Canary Islands a few months ago. It read, “These islands aren’t for everyone. Do you practice responsible tourism? If so, you’re welcome in the Canary Islands.”
