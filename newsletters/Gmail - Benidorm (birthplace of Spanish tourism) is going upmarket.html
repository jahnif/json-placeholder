Benidorm (birthplace of Spanish tourism) is going upmarket

Benidorm now:

How'd that happen?

In the 1950s, Benidorm's mayor was granted permission by Franco and the Church to transform the struggling fishing village into a tourist haven.

The plan was a success, transforming the city into a hotspot for tourists, particularly from Northern Europe and the UK, for many years to come.

Now, it seems that Benidorm, similar to other Spanish party destinations like Magaluf, is planning to go upscale.

Twenty hotels are in the pipeline, with a focus on the four and 5-star bracket.

I learned this in this article from Spain-based journalist Graham Keeley.​

To be honest, Benidorm’s planned “upgrade” doesn't surprise me at all.

The twin beaches are stunning, and the weather is consistently excellent.

Yoly and I visited a few years ago, bracing ourselves for the worst.

I was expecting the place to be tacky and depressing.

But to my surprise, I was low-key impressed.

It’s easy to dismiss Spanish longtime sun & sand destinations like Benidorm as a bit tawdry.

However, doing so overlooks the cultural and historical significance that the decades-old tourism boom in Spain has brought.

Despite its reputation, Benidorm is a vibrant British exclave and a beloved tourist destination, making it uniquely valuable.

This realization dawned on me during a conversation with Madrid-based British journalist Leah Pattem over coffee.

Having just returned from Benidorm, she had penned an article for El País (which you can easily translate using the Chrome Google Translate extension) that pays tribute to Ray's Chippy, the oldest fish and chip shop in Benidorm and possibly in Spain.

Established in 1979, it primarily serves British clients. However, Leah noted an increasing number of Spaniards dining there.

While some may view Benidorm's shift towards luxury as a positive move, I hope the city doesn't lose the unique characteristics that make it Benidorm (whether you're a fan or not).

In other words, I hope Ray's Chippy — a restaurant older than many in Spain and filled with countless memories — withstands this upscale transformation.

For now, I find myself with a sudden craving for fish and chips.
