Is American tipping culture taking over Spain?

So, this happened the other day…

I was meeting a friend for coffee in a trendy Madrid coffee shop, and I got hit with an American-style tipping screen.

Yes, we tip in Spain.

But I had never seen this before.

Let me explain.

In this coffee shop, you walk in and order at the counter, then sit down, and they bring your coffee and food to you.

I ordered two coffees and a sandwich.

When it came time to pay, she explained that a tip screen would appear.

I panicked and did two things:

  • Took this photo
  • Left a 10% tip 🤣

I guess it’s a personality thing, but in moments of stress like this, I tend to take the route of least offense.

But in the future, I won’t be tipping this way in Spain again.

Here’s why…

In Spain, the custom is to leave a small tip if the service was good. While waiters don’t earn a lot, they are not “living” off tips, like in the US. They earn a salary. Tips are structured as a bonus.

What’s crazy about this is:

  • I hadn’t even received the service yet, or tasted the food, but I was being asked to tip. So there was no way I could leave a tip based on my assessment of my experience.
  • We don’t think about tipping as a percentage in Spain. It’s a little bit of change you leave at the end of your meal.

Why does this matter? Am I just being cheap?

I think it matters because if this tipping style grows in Spain, it takes away the responsibility from business owners to pay a fair salary to their employees.

Effectively, it shifts the onus onto the customer to pay the salary, as per the US model.

Which, in my opinion, is simply wrong.

What’s more, how much you’re paid in Spain is tied to how much your employer pays into the social security system on your behalf.

This means if you’re paid less (because the weight of your salary is shifted onto tips), then you get less unemployment protection, and you’re paying less into your pension.

In short, the entire social welfare system is built on the idea that you are paid a fair wage by your employer.

I haven’t seen this elsewhere, and I hope it doesn’t grow.

But if you encounter this screen in Spain, I recommend hitting “no tip” and following the local tipping tradition.

I’ll be sure to do so next time.
