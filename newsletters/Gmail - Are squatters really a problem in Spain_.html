Are squatters really a problem in Spain?

It's a homeowner's worst nightmare.

You come home from holiday, and people are living in your home.

And there is so much chatter and anecdotal horror stories online about people's homes being squatted that it's enough to make you second-guess moving to Spain or buying a home here.

But what does the data actually show?

Because beyond the horror stories and dramatic headlines, it can be difficult to find actual data in English about the prevalence of squatting.

So, I want to provide you with the data so you can make an informed decision about the likelihood of this happening to you.

First, let's get a couple of things out of the way:

  1. Yes, squatting happens. In 2022 (the last year I could get reliable data for), there were just under 17,000 police complaints for squatting (out of 26,000,000 homes in Spain, for context).
  2. Without diving into the socio-economic situation that drives many people to occupy homes, I want to make clear that I believe squatting is wrong and should never happen.

*It's important for me to get those first two out of the way because whenever I bring data to this conversation, I get attacked for denying squatting happens and/or for condoning it.

But now, let's dig into the data.

Because trust me, the devil is in the detail.

First, there is no such crime as "squatting" in Spain.

There are, in fact, two different crimes, each covering different circumstances.

They are:

  • Allanamiento de morada, and
  • Usurpación de inmueble

The first type is the least common (only 5% of squatting is this type), and the second type is more common (95% of situations fit into this category).

Let's discuss the first type (it's the type that's most relevant to you).

Type 1: Allanamiento de morada

This is the crime someone commits if they occupy a home that is inhabited (by you, for example).

Inhabited can mean it's your primary residence or a second/vacation home.

In these cases, you can get rid of the squatters with a police order if you act quickly.

While it's not legally clear how quickly you need to act (the law doesn't state any timeframe, but jurisprudence suggests 48 hours, though others suggest longer is ok), the key is that you (or an authorized person) report the occupation to the police with proof that you are the owner of the property.

The police will remove the squatters practically immediately.

In this case, there is no need for a long judicial process.

But here is the key...

Only 5% of occupations are this type (more about why below).

Type 2: Usurpación de inmueble

This is the crime someone commits if they occupy a property that is uninhabited (such as an empty property owned by a bank).

Keep in mind that there are almost 4 million empty properties in Spain, so there are many of these types of properties.

In this case, a judicial order is needed to remove the squatters, and this can take time (because the court process can be slow).

This type of squatting accounted for 95% of the reported cases in 2022.

This makes sense: when people squat a place, they usually want to stay there. And they're more likely to do that successfully if nobody else lives there.

Additionally, the punishment for this type of squatting (empty properties) is lighter than for the other type.

So overall, it's understandable why 95% of cases of squatting are in uninhabited properties.

Takeaways

If you're buying a home in Spain (whether a primary residence or vacation home), the only type of squatting that would apply to you is type 1.

And this only happened to approximately 850 properties out of the 26,000,000 in Spain in 2022 (5% of 17,000 reported cases).

Yes, I'm sure there were more that were unreported, but I'm also quite sure most people who have their primary or holiday residence occupied report it to the police.

And if, in the very unlikely event that your home is occupied, as long as you act quickly, the police will remove them immediately.

If it provides you with a sense of security, then by all means, get an alarm for your home. We have an alarm in our apartment (not for squatting, but because I have a lot of camera gear).

So, while it would be a nightmare if it happened, I think the data shows that it's very unlikely to happen.

But of course, now that you have actual data, you don't have to take anyone's word for it (mine included); you can make an informed decision for yourself.

It's important to mention that "squatting" also includes situations where you rent a place out, and the tenants who are living there stop paying rent. I don't have data on this type of situation, but most of the people in this community aren't looking to buy a place to offer it as a long-term rental.
