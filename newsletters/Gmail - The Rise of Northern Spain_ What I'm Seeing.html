I’m standing in a village in Asturias in 2014, surrounded by cows and rolling hills, and all I can think is:

This feels like New Zealand.

The air is damp. Everything’s green.

I whipped out my iPhone to capture the scene.

I’d been living in Spain for three years. And this wasn’t the Spain I knew.

Up until then, my experience of Spain had been light, dry, sharp-edged. Sun on stone. The yellow tones of Castilla. The glare of a Valencian afternoon.

But here… everything was softened.

I’ve been helping people move to Spain for four years now. Long enough to start noticing patterns.

And shifts.

And for a long time, the focus was clear: the costas on the south and east — with a dabbling of Madrid and Barcelona.

That’s still true.

But more and more, I’m hearing a different kind of question from this audience:

“Could we live in Galicia?”
“I’m drawn to the north… though my husband’s not sure.”
“We’ve walked the Camino four times… the north is where our heart is.”

(And it’s exactly the kind of thing we’ll be diving into in a free webinar next week — more on that below.)

In short, it’s not just curiosity. It’s a current. And it’s gaining strength.

I’ve commented in past newsletters about how tourism in the north is growing. Places like Galicia and Asturias are seeing more visitors each year.

Last July, Asturias had more national tourists than Málaga… for the first time in history.

But lately, it’s not just tourists.

Between 2022 and late 2024, Asturias and Galicia ranked first and second in all of Spain for growth in foreign residents, as a percentage.

Just behind them was Castilla y León.

Yep, more and more people are moving to northern Spain.

And it’s a striking turn of fortune for the north.

Galicia, Asturias, and Castilla y León have long been threatened by rural depopulation and an aging population.

In short, these are regions that, for decades, were losing people.

So what’s drawing people north?

From the people I speak to in this audience and my Move to Spain Masterclass, here’s what keeps coming up:

  • Milder summers. Some people are looking for somewhere more temperate.
  • Affordability. Prices in many northern towns and villages are still accessible.
  • Cultural immersion. Fewer foreign resident bubbles. More motivation to speak Spanish, to integrate.
  • The Camino. A surprising number of people want to stay connected to that journey. Some even want to live along the route.
  • Nature. Green hills. Wild coastlines. Rain. Trees. Not everyone wants endless sun.

But there’s a trade-off.

The north comes with a mood.

Winters can be wet. Days can be gray. The rain doesn’t just visit — it settles in.

When I was in Asturias recently, I spoke to a woman who’d moved from Madrid.

She said she loved it.

Then she leaned in a little, gave a half-smile, and said:

“The rain is real.”

As always, it’s not about right or wrong.

It comes down to vision.

What kind of life do you want to build here? What sustains you?
