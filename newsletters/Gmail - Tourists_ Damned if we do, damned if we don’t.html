"I feel a bit guilty bringing you here," Edu said, half-joking. "This tapas bar is so local."

That comment stuck with me.

It was what Edu told me while I was filming my latest video, where we explored some of the most local tapas bars and restaurants in Barcelona’s neighborhoods.

My passion is celebrating great food spots—and the people who make them.

But when I’m filming food videos, especially those featuring off-the-beaten-path places, I wrestle with a dilemma.

If I only feature touristy spots, what’s the point? That’s not even a real picture of Spain.

But if I highlight more local places, am I contributing to over-tourism?

And I know I’m not alone in this.

Tourists get criticized for sticking to the obvious spots—places that cater to them with oversized sangrias and tourist-trap menus.

But then, when they seek out the real, authentic places, they’re told they’re ruining them.

It puts travelers in a strange position—trying to be respectful but never quite getting it right.

So where does that leave us?

What does ethical travel actually look like?

If you love Spain (or anywhere) and want to visit responsibly, you might be wondering:

What are the thoughtful choices we can make to support local culture—without accidentally eroding it?

I want to make a video that dives into this, with real, practical ideas. Not just the usual "be polite" or "learn a few Spanish words" advice—but the less obvious, more meaningful stuff.

So I’d love your perspective.

But first, here are a few ideas I’ve been thinking about lately—just to get us started:

​

1. Seek out dishes that actually represent a place. 🥘

While filming my recent video in Mallorca, my guide Alex pointed something out that I had never considered before.

Many restaurants aren’t serving true Mallorcan food anymore.

Instead, they offer generic tapas and paella—because that’s what tourists expect.

And it hit me: if local dishes disappear from menus, the culture behind them starts to disappear too.

One simple but powerful choice: look for the hyper-local dishes, the ones that aren’t on every menu, the ones locals still cook at home.

That’s how we help keep food traditions alive.

2. Be conscious of how you stay 🏠

Let’s talk about Airbnb.

It’s well known that short-term rentals drive up housing costs for locals (​this recent study​ showed that vacation rentals have driven rental prices in Malaga up 31%).

And it’s not just Málaga—this is happening in cities all over the world.

But here’s the thing: Airbnb is convenient.

It’s great for families or groups traveling together. It often feels more affordable. And from a pure supply-and-demand perspective, having more beds available can actually help keep overall accommodation prices lower.

We’ve seen this in places like New York—when Airbnb is restricted, hotel prices go up. So even well-intentioned policies can have unintended consequences.

So does ethical travel mean spending more? Can everyone afford to do that?

And if ethical travel does cost more, does that mean fewer people should travel—or that only wealthier travelers get to feel “responsible”?

There are no easy answers here. But maybe the takeaway isn’t about always avoiding Airbnb—it’s about being conscious of where and how we use it.

In tourism-heavy neighborhoods where locals are being priced out, maybe it’s worth choosing locally owned hotels, small guesthouses, or places outside the pressure zones.

It’s not about being perfect. It’s about making more intentional choices—when and where we can.

​

3. Remember: local businesses have agency too. 🍽️

Take Casa Toni in Madrid. It’s one of my favorite places, and I’ve featured it in multiple videos (including this one below).

It’s one of the last old-school bars in the historic center. It’s packed with both locals and tourists.

And yet, after all these years:

  • Prices are still low.
  • The place is still rustic, informal and welcoming
  • Literally nothing has changed in the last 10 years (though yes, I wish they'd do up the bathrooms...)

They’ve chosen to stay true to their roots.

Sometimes, it’s not about avoiding local places, but supporting the ones that want to welcome both locals and travelers—without compromising their identity or values.

​

4. Be mindful of local resources and waste. ♻️

Spain suffers from droughts—take shorter showers, reuse towels, and avoid unnecessary laundry.

There’s also the question of waste.

As tourists, we sometimes opt out of the habits we follow at home—like sorting our recycling or being mindful of plastic use.

Ask where you’re staying about how waste and recycling work, rather than just tossing everything in the same bin.

Show hotel owners that responsible waste management matters to visitors as much as to locals.
