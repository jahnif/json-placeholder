No, there is no "tourist ban"

Every morning, I get a Google alert serving up the top headlines about Spain from the last 24 hours.

And for the last week, I've noticed a bizarre trend.

"Spain's 'tourist ban' – what you need to know"

"Spain's three-year 'tourist ban' – and what it means"

"Spain introduces three-year tourist ban..."

Wait—what tourist ban?

A quick click-through to the articles, and I realized these headlines were, well, fake news.

What they were really talking about was Spain cracking down on tourist rentals in response to the housing crisis.

Specifically, Málaga has introduced a three-year freeze on new tourist rentals in neighborhoods where such rentals make up more than 8% of the housing stock.

So, in short—no tourist ban.

But beyond the misleading headlines, there’s something deeper bubbling under the surface.

I've seen complaints online about how Spain’s crackdown on short-term rentals might lead to higher accommodation prices.

But these rants never seem to acknowledge that local people are struggling—and that something has to give.

At the heart of the "tourist ban" panic is an attitude of "How dare you mess with our favorite holiday destination?"

"We want things just as they've always been, and you have no right to mess with that."

I even get messages in my DMs from people claiming that limiting Airbnb—by regulating or restricting short-term rentals—is Spain sliding into communism.

As if attempting to manage the explosive growth of (often illegal and unlicensed) tourist rentals is just one step away from food stamps.

I’m no historian, but it all seems a bit of a stretch.

Especially given that Spain’s economic growth reached 3.2% in 2024, and the unemployment rate fell to 10.6%, the lowest since 2008.

But what all these reactions have in common is a sense that some people feel threatened by Spain’s efforts (or at least its signals) that it wants to rein in the housing crisis.

For now, I just hope Spain can lead the way in balancing the needs of both tourists and residents—because as global tourism keeps growing, a new model isn’t just preferable… it’s necessary.

And as tourists—curious to explore, understand, and honor the cultures we visit—we should celebrate that.
